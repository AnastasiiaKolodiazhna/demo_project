Geyser Fly, USA

It is hard to believe, but this “fountain”, as if descended from the pages of a science fiction writer’s book, is not on Jupiter, not on Mars, but on Earth, in the state of Nevada. The “flying” geyser spews streams of hot water to a height of 15 meters, forming around itself a “mini-volcano” of mineral sediments. Scientists say that something like this looked like the surface of our planet millions of years ago. The geyser is located on the territory of a private ranch, and to admire it, you need permission of the owner. But tourists do not stop. People believe that if you wash water from a geyser, life will be bright and happy.

Devil's Tower, USA

Devil's Tower, or Devils Tower - is a columnar mountain in Wyoming. It resembles a tower assembled from individual columns. It is hard to believe that this is a creation of nature, and not of human hands. The indigenous population belonged to the tower with reverent horror, because at the top many times observed strange light phenomena. There is a legend that the devil sits on the top and beats the drum, causing thunder. Due to bad reputation mountain climbers bypass the mountain. But she appears in the film Steven Spielberg's "Close contacts of the third degree" - this is where the meeting with the aliens takes place.

Troll Language, Norway

Trolltunga, or Troll's Tongue, is an unusual stone ledge at an altitude of 350 meters on the Skieggeddal mountain. Why language? And why troll? As the old Norwegian legend says, in those parts there lived a troll who constantly experienced fate: he dived into deep pools, jumping through precipices. One day he decided to check whether it was true that the rays of the sun are deadly for trolls. At dawn, he stuck out his tongue from his cave and ... was petrified forever. The rock like a magnet attracts modern adventurers: sit on the edge, make a flip, take a picture. There is no troll, but his work lives!

Stonehenge, United Kingdom

130 km from London, in the county of Wiltshire, is a fancy building of huge stone blocks. This is one of the most popular archaeological sites in the world. The researchers found that the construction of the complex lasted almost two thousand years and took place in several stages. However, it is still unclear who built it and why. According to folk legend, the huge blue stones have magical powers, and the building was erected by a wizard named Merlin. There is also a version that Stonehenge is a stone age observatory, a Druid sanctuary or an ancient tomb.

Horstale Falls, United States

In Yosemite National Park, on the eastern slope of Mount El Capitan, there is a 650-meter waterfall. Most of the year, it is unremarkable, but in February, the falling streams of water turn into "lava flows." An amazing natural phenomenon is due to the fact that at sunset the sun's rays are reflected in the waterfall, creating a visual illusion that the red-hot metal flows down from the rock. According to legend, on the top of the mountain was the home of a blacksmith, who made the best horseshoes for horses in the area. But because of the heavy rain, the smithy was washed off the cliff. Since then, the waterfall once a year "reminds" of this tragic event.

Loch Ness, UK

Loch Ness is a deep lake in the highlands of Scotland, where, according to legend, the monster lives. Ostensibly this creature resembling a prehistoric pangolin. One of the eyewitnesses described it this way: 40 feet long, 4 fins, the torso smoothly passes into an elongated neck with small tubercles. There are quite a few people who claim to have seen the Loch Ness monster. More than three thousand cases have been recorded. There is even a photo and video footage. But there are enough skeptics. The debate about whether there is a monster in the lake lasts for decades, and from time to time it flares up with a new force.

Big Blue Hole, Belize

This is a huge funnel with a diameter of 305 meters and a depth of 120 meters. Located in the center of Lighthouse Reef. In 1972, Jacques-Yves Cousteau established that originally it was a system of limestone caves that originated during the ice age. When the ocean level rose, the cave roof collapsed and a karst crater formed. But there is an opinion that flooding could not provoke destruction - too big sizes, too correct round shape. There should have been an external impact, for example, the fall of a meteorite.
